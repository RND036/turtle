    Sri Lanka

    INTRODUCTION

    Sri Lanka shelters a good count of endangered species across the globe, and one of the most exciting species among them is the sea turtle and also it is considered as a national gem. Sri Lanka is home to several species of sea turtles, including the Green turtle, Olive Ridley turtle, Hawksbill turtle, Loggerhead turtle, and Leatherback turtle. To protect Sri Lanka's sea turtles, including the establishment of marine protected areas and hatcheries to protect turtle nests and hatchlings from predators and human activities. The Sri Lankan government has also implemented regulations to prohibit the killing, harming, or trading of sea turtles and their eggs Turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka are conservation projects that aim to protect sea turtle populations and their habitats. These hatcheries are usually run by non-profit organizations and local communities that work to protect and conserve sea turtles by collecting eggs laid by female turtles on the beaches, hatching them in a protected environment, and releasing the hatchlings back into the ocean. Visitors to turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka can learn about the life cycle of sea turtles, observe hatchlings, and even participate in the release of baby turtles back into the ocean. It is important to note that not all turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka operate ethically, and some may contribute to the exploitation of turtles. Therefore, it is crucial to do your research and choose a responsible and reputable turtle hatchery to visit.

    SEA TURTLES OF SRI LANKA

    Sea turtles in Sri Lanka are a significant and cherished part of the country's marine ecosystem. Five species of sea turtles can be found nesting and foraging along the coastline of Sri Lanka:

    1. Green Turtle: The green turtle is one of the largest species of turtle in the world and can weigh up to 200 kg.They have a distinctive greenish-yellow color and a smooth carapace (upper shell). They are herbivores and feed on seagrasses and algae.
    2. Olive Ridley Turtle: Olive ridley turtles are smaller in size than green turtles and have a more rounded shell. They are known for their synchronized nesting behavior, where thousands of females come together to lay their eggs on a single beach. They feed on crustaceans, mollusks, and jellyfish.
    3. Hawksbill Turtle: Hawksbill turtles are smaller in size and have a unique pattern of overlapping scales on their carapace that resembles the pattern of a tortoiseshell. They are carnivorous and feed on sponges, anemones, and jellyfish.
    4. Loggerhead Turtle: The loggerhead turtle has a large head and strong jaw, which it uses to crush the hard shells of its prey, such as crabs and clams. They have a reddish-brown color and a distinctive heart-shaped carapace.
    5. Leatherback Turtle: The leatherback turtle is the largest of all sea turtles and can grow up to 2 meters in length. They have a unique soft, rubbery carapace that lacks the hard bony plates found in other turtle species. They are also unique in that they feed almost exclusively on jellyfish.

    THE HATCHERIES WORKING STYLES

    • Staff patrols beach at nights looking for mother sea turtles that come out of the sea to lay eggs.
    • They protect the mother from afar during the nesting process, until she finishes laying the eggs and returns to the ocean.
    • The eggs are relocated from beach to hatchery to protect them from predators and irresponsible human action.
    • In addition fishermen are also paid to bring any turtle eggs they find to the hatchery.
    • The eggs develop and hatch in a protected area. This may take between 45-60 days.
    • The hatched turtles are released into the ocean on the same day if no complications arise. This is to avoid human interference in their lifestyles as much as possible.
